# UI/UX Proposal: Immersive Clinical POV

## 1. Visual Philosophy: "The Clinical Gaze"
The UI should not feel like a game menu; it should feel like you are sitting in a therapist's chair. Every element should have a physical or thematic justification.

## 2. The Office Viewport (The Core UX)
Instead of a flat background, the session takes place within a 16:9 "window" into the office.
- **Perspective:** First-person POV looking slightly down at a patient sitting in a chair.
- **Atmosphere:** Dynamic lighting (day/night) and subtle environmental details (dust motes, clock ticking).
- **Depth:** Parallax layers for the background (wall), middle ground (patient), and foreground (therapist's desk edge).

## 3. Interactive UI Elements

### A. The Physical Clipboard
- **Behavior:** Slides in from the bottom of the screen when the player wants to view collected tokens or the handbook.
- **Aesthetic:** High-resolution texture of aged paper and a metal clip.
- **Interaction:** Drag-and-drop tokens to synthesize breakthroughs.

### B. The Response Wheel
- **Behavior:** Appears when it's the player's turn to speak.
- **Aesthetic:** A circular menu with soft, clinical colors. Each segment represents a technique (e.g., "Clarify" is blue, "Challenge" is red).
- **UX:** Minimalist text until hovered/tapped to reveal the full response.

### C. The Certification Wall
- **Behavior:** Background elements that the player can click on between sessions.
- **Progression:** Initially a bare wall. As the player unlocks specializations, framed certificates appear.
- **Detail:** Clicking a certificate zooms in to show the player's name and the specific achievement.

## 4. User Experience (UX) Flow

### A. Session Flow
1. **The Entrance:** Patient walks in and sits down (fade from black).
2. **Active Listening:** Patient speaks; text appears in a subtle bubble near their head.
3. **The Pivot:** Player toggles "Focus Mode" to observe body language. The screen darkens except for the patient.
4. **Synthesis:** Player opens the clipboard to combine a "Keyword" (what they said) with a "Visual Cue" (how they acted).

### B. Documentation UX
- Transition from the immersive office to a "Workstation" view.
- Drag-and-drop symptoms into a digital patient file.
- The UI mimics a professional medical record software (clean, high-contrast, serif fonts).

## 5. Visual Cues & Feedback
- **Anxiety Meter:** Represented by a subtle "heart rate" monitor or a glass tube on the desk.
- **Haptic Feedback:** Short vibrations on mobile when a breakthrough token is discovered.
- **Typography:** 
    - *Serif (Georgia):* Used for patient dialogue and official reports to convey weight and tradition.
    - *Sans-Serif (Inter):* Used for system menus and technical data.
